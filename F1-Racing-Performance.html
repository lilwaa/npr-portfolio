Introduction

Formula 1 also known as F1, is the most popular car racing sport in the world. With each competition (known as a grand prix) lasting three days, there is a practice round, a qualifying round to determine the starting positions, and the actual race. The race, held on the last day is 305 kilometers long, with the winner being the driver/car who completes the set number of laps that make up this distance and reaches the finish line the fastest. With each race occurring in a different country with a different route the settings of the races greatly vary. Drivers are ranked by points accumulated from each grand prix, with first place awarding the most points. At the end of each year, the driver with the most points is named the world champion.

The popularity of this sport makes predicting winners either for fun, gambling purposes, or for the participants knowledge, highly coveted information. To increase the accuracy of these predictions multiple factors were compared to the winners of each grand prix and the world championship to determine any correlations. In this analysis, the age and nationality of the drivers, qualifying positions, pit stops, and car constructor standing were all compared to the winning positions to determine if a relationship was present.

Data Description

The data used included the results, drivers, driver standings, constructor, constructor standings,races, qualifying, and pitstops datasets. The results data set was the one that all other data connected back to, as it contains information on the final positions of each driver in the grand prix. It has 25840 observations and 18 columns. The drivers dataset was used to obtain personal information about the drivers and contains 857 entries with 9 columns. The driver standings dataset was used to obtain information about the performance of the drivers in each rave, with 33902 observations and 7 columns. The constructors dataset was used to obtain information about the different car constructor's such as their name and nationality, it has 211 observations with 5 columns. The constructor standings dataset gave information on the performance of cars made by different constructor's and contains 12941 observations with 7 columns. The races dataset gives information about when each grandprix took place and on what circuit. It contains 1102 observations with 18 columns. The qualifying dataset gives information on the drivers qualifying races that occur as a preliminary round for each grand prix. It contains 9575 observations and 9 columns. Finally the pitstops dataset gives information on the number and length of pitstops taken by each participant in the races. It contains 9634 observations and 7 columns.

Merging & Calculation Procedures

Columns that identified races, drivers, constructors and their positions were selected from the results dataset. Columns that we hypothesized could relate to the final position of drivers were selected and merged using the pd.merge function from pandas into the results dataset. All datasets used were linked by either raceId, driverId, or constructorIs. Any time a merged column had the same name as an existing column in the results dataset, the columns were renamed. For pitstops, the total number of pitstops was first calculated before it was merged with constructor name, race year, and final finishing time from results. For pits stops the average number of stops, time, stop length for each constructor of each race using aggregate statistics. The year, raceId, and name of the constructor are kept in this final dataframe by keeping the first value that fits in this group. The final number of observations of constructor race results with pitstops is 1561. The columns are renamed accordingly as averages.

To examine the overall performance of both the drivers and constructors for the world championship and constructor's championship their total points over the years was examined. At the same time, the average stop length for each constructor was between 1.4e4 and 2.05e6 ms, with the average of all constructors at 2.36e4 ms.

To analyze how the qualifying positions relate to the actual positions, we created two visualizations: a hexbin plot and a box plot.

The hexbin plot shows the correlation between qualifying positions and actual race positions. Each hexagon in the plot represents a grouping of data points, with the x-axis showing the qualifying positions and the y-axis showing the actual race positions. The color of the hexagon corresponds to the number of data points within that region, with darker colors indicating higher concentrations of data points. We can observe a general trend that higher qualifying positions (i.e., closer to 1) tend to result in better race positions. However, there is still some variability, indicating that qualifying position is not the sole determinant of race performance.

To determine if there is a relationship between the number of pitstops and race performance a plot was made comparing pit stops to total race time by constructor.

Comparing the correlation between number of pit stops to finish time, we used an OLS regression to determine the relationship. On average, the finishing time can be estimated by 4.45e5 * number of pits stops + 5.105e6. The positive slope suggests that a greater number of pits stops corresponds to a slower finishing time. The adjusted r squared value is 0.123 indicating that 12.3% of the variation in the finishing time is due to the change in the number of pit stops. The probability of the F-statistic is 9.73e-47, below the alpha value of 0.05 indicating that there is evidence of a relationship between the number of pit stops and the finishing time. From these statistics and the graph where we can see the positive trendline for each constructor, we conclude that there is some effect of the number of pit stops on the finishing time and that constructors who take less pit stops, have a faster finishing time.

Discussion

We can observe a general trend that higher qualifying positions (i.e., closer to 1) tend to result in better race positions. In particular, the darkest hexagons are clustered around the bottom-left corner of the plot, suggesting that drivers who qualify in the first position often secure the victory in the race. However, there is still some variability, indicating that qualifying position is not the sole determinant of race performance. The relationship between driver’s points and therefore performance of the top drivers seems to be somewhat sporadic. With no obvious correlation between the top drivers points over the last 5 years it seems that this data would not be helpful in predicting the winners of the following years. However, the data did show that drivers who have gotten a high number of points in past seasons are likely to continue finishing in the top 10 positions of each grand prix, making it so they accumulate some number of points each season, even if it is not enough to win. The constructors on the other hand, have a lot of overlap for their points within the last 5 years, indicating that the top constructors are relatively stable in their dominance of the sport. Certain aspects of the driver’s biography did seem to be common among the winner. For example, the British and German seemed to have a rather consistent winning streak, and the Dutch have become a rather strong competitor for the top position in recent years. However, the age the driver did not influence the their placement within the top few positions. All the top positions had a mean age of around 30, and the difference in mean age between the first and rest of the top positions was not large. The number of pitstops was found to have a positive correlation with finishing time, indicating that constructors who have the need to take more pit stops on average to not perform as well as those who take fewer. If the average number of pit stops a constructor’s car takes is examined then this can be used to help predict the position that they will get in the grand prix. Overall it was found that some factors such as qualifying position, constructor, nationality, and number of pitstops are correlated with the winners of F1 races and therefore championships. However it was also found that some factors such as age had no correlation. This data can be useful when trying to predict the winners as an outside viewer, or when perfecting a winning strategy as a driver/constructor.
